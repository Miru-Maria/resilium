## Executive Summary

The consumer personal resilience and preparedness market remains thinly competitive at the product layer. Most incumbents occupy one of three limiting positions: government checklists (static, no personalization), B2B wellness platforms (enterprise-only, no consumer path), or hobby-level content aggregators (no score, no action plan, no subscription model). None offer the combination Resilium delivers — a scored, AI-personalized, multi-dimensional resilience assessment with an actionable plan at a consumer price point.

The biggest funded threat — BetterUp — is firmly B2B and shows no clear consumer pivot. The most format-similar competitor — ReadyScore — is deepening its survivalist niche rather than pursuing mainstream users. The space Resilium occupies is genuinely unclaimed by any well-resourced operator.

## Competitor Profiles

### 1. ReadyScore — `readyscore.com`
**Priority: High (direct format match)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Score-based emergency preparedness platform covering 11 categories (water, food, shelter, power, financial, communications, health, security, transportation, documents, community). Free assessment, no paywall — business model unclear.

**Strengths:**
- Concept validation: a scored preparedness platform is a legitimate product category
- Strong survivalist / prepper brand credibility
- Recently published "Prep Like Noah" book (expanding brand)
- Copyright updated to 2026 — actively maintained

**Weaknesses:**
- Survivalist framing ("Supply chains are fragile. The grid is vulnerable.") alienates mainstream users — the quietly anxious professional won't self-identify as a prepper
- No Pro tier, no paywall — no clear monetization; funding risk
- No mobile app — web only
- No AI personalization — static scoring rubric, no scenario modeling
- No psychological resilience dimension — purely practical/material
- No action plan generation — outputs a score, not a roadmap
- No email engagement loop, no drip sequence, no lifecycle automation

**What Resilium does differently:** Mainstream framing ("know your readiness" vs "prepare for collapse"), psychological + financial dimensions, AI-generated personalized action plan, Pro subscription model, mobile app, email automation, and scenario stress-testing. We sell peace of mind to normies; they sell preparedness to preppers.

**Monitoring trigger:** ReadyScore launches a Pro/paid tier or a mobile app → accelerate mainstream SEO content; publish direct comparison content.

### 2. BetterUp — `betterup.com` / `betterup.com/products/betterup-ready`
**Priority: High (well-funded, B2B resilience intensifying)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Enterprise coaching and mental fitness platform. In 2025, launched **BetterUp Ready™** — a dedicated workforce resilience product with human + AI coaching, "Personal Concierge," and longitudinal ROI tracking for HR teams. B2B-only.

**Strengths:**
- Significant funding (hundreds of millions); enterprise sales motion
- Strong scientific credibility and brand recognition in HR/L&D space
- BetterUp Ready™ is a genuine, well-built product with coaching depth
- Network of 3,000+ certified coaches

**Weaknesses:**
- No consumer path — requires enterprise purchase via sales team
- Pricing is opaque and enterprise-level ($30–$60+/user/month); completely different tier
- No self-serve individual subscription
- Workforce resilience framing is narrow — doesn't address financial, material, or geographic dimensions
- No emergency preparedness, crisis guide, or scenario stress-test component
- Platform is coaching-heavy, not data/score-heavy — no assessable output you can act on independently

**Threat scenario:** BetterUp announces a self-serve individual tier at consumer price point. This would bring their brand, coaching network, and funding into direct competition. Probability: Low in 12 months. Monitoring: LinkedIn job postings for "consumer," "B2C," "self-service" roles.

### 3. Coached.com — `coached.com`
**Priority: Medium-Low (largely exited resilience space)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Originally launched as a resilience/personal development platform (1.2M+ users). **Pivoted in 2025** to a career and personality assessment tool ("uncomfortably accurate"). Resilience tool demoted to `/tools/` as a secondary page.

**Strengths (residual):**
- 1.2M registered users — the distribution exists, even if dormant on resilience
- Quality UX from original build
- Brand familiarity in the self-improvement space

**Weaknesses:**
- Mission drift: current brand has nothing to do with resilience
- No active investment in resilience product
- Personality/career framing doesn't overlap with Resilium's anxiety/preparedness angle

**Threat scenario:** Coached.com pivots back to resilience, leveraging their 1.2M user base. Would be the most dangerous competitive development. Monitoring: brand mentions + new feature launches.

### 4. Driven Resilience (PR6) — `home.hellodriven.com`
**Priority: Medium (expanding to individuals, US reach limited)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Originally B2B workplace resilience consulting. Rebranded to "Driven Resilience" from "Hello Driven." Now sells individual resilience courses and certifications via a shop (AUD pricing). Scientific credibility via the PR6 (Personal Resilience Framework).

**Strengths:**
- PR6 has academic credibility — referenced in peer literature
- Brand has been around since 2019; not a flash-in-the-pan
- Expanding from B2B to individual consumer model

**Weaknesses:**
- AUD pricing — not yet targeting US/European consumers meaningfully
- Course/certification model (high-touch, high-price) vs. Resilium's SaaS subscription (always-on, accessible)
- No real-time personalized scoring — courses are pre-built modules, not adaptive
- No emergency preparedness or material resilience dimension
- No mobile app or platform

**Threat scenario:** Driven Resilience launches a USD consumer subscription or self-serve app. Their scientific credibility + PR6 framework could be compelling. Currently low probability. Monitoring: shop/pricing page for USD toggle.

### 5. IDR Labs — `idrlabs.com`
**Priority: Low (static aggregator, no product ambition)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Free psychology test aggregator. Offers a "Personal Resilience Scale" test at `/personal-resilience/test.php`. No subscription model, no action plan, no mobile app.

**Strengths:**
- High organic search traffic (SEO moat from breadth of test content)
- Used by academics and curious individuals
- Free, zero friction — captures top-of-funnel users

**Weaknesses:**
- No monetization model — not a competitor in any commercial sense
- Output is a static score with a brief text interpretation; no personalization, no action plan
- No follow-up, no lifecycle, no engagement loop
- Test is purely psychological — no financial, practical, or material dimensions

**Strategic note:** IDR Labs is a lead-generation opportunity disguised as a competitor. Users who find IDR Labs' resilience test and want more are a natural Resilium audience. A content piece targeting "personal resilience scale test" could capture this traffic directly.

### 6. FEMA / Red Cross — `ready.gov` / `redcross.org`
**Priority: Low (government/nonprofit, different mission)**
**Updated: April 2026**

**What they do:** Government-issued emergency preparedness content. Static guides, checklists, and mobile apps (FEMA app, Red Cross Emergency app).

**Strengths:**
- Trust and authority by default — government imprimatur
- Free; massively distributed via public campaigns
- Wide awareness, especially after disaster events (storm season, earthquake, etc.)

**Weaknesses:**
- No personalized scoring — content is generic, not adaptive
- No AI, no scenario modeling, no financial/psychological dimension
- Government content is by nature slow-moving, bureaucratic, low-design
- App experience is functional but dated — no engagement loop

**Strategic note:** FEMA / Red Cross create awareness of preparedness without solving it in a personalized way. They are market educators, not competitors. The Resilium user often starts with FEMA.gov and realizes it's inadequate — that's the handoff moment.

## Resilium's Defensible Advantages

### 1. The only scored, AI-personalized consumer resilience platform
No competitor combines personalized multi-dimensional scoring + AI-generated action plan + scenario stress-testing in a self-serve subscription at consumer price. This is the core white space.

### 2. Breadth of dimensions is a moat
ReadyScore covers practical preparedness. BetterUp covers psychological wellness. Driven Resilience covers mindset. Resilium is the only platform that integrates all six: Financial · Skills · Health · Mobility · Psychological · Resources. The cross-dimension insight ("your financial buffer partially compensates for your low social support") is unique.

### 3. Mobile-first engagement loop
No meaningful competitor has a native mobile app with daily check-ins, streak tracking, push notifications, and offline crisis guides. Mobile creates a habitual engagement surface that web-only competitors cannot replicate.

### 4. Mainstream vs. prepper framing
ReadyScore owns the prepper/survivalist niche. Resilium explicitly targets the "quietly anxious" mainstream — financially literate, educated, not self-identified as a prepper. This framing is currently unoccupied and is a significantly larger addressable market.

### 5. Data moat (compounding)
As assessment volume grows, percentile benchmarks become more meaningful and harder to replicate. "Your financial resilience is in the top 23% of users in your income bracket" is a value proposition that only exists with data.
